Meet Catalan Number

The very first time Catalan number and I met each other, I was taking the data structure course provided by Tsinghua University. The question is: how many Binary Search Trees (BST) can one build with 3 nodes? The answer is 5, the same as the third number of the Catalan number series. OK but what is Catalan number?

Two ways of understanding Catalan Number

The first one

There are two ways of expressing Catalan number, so I develop two understanding of it.

The first one is:

What does this mean? In one scentence: it presents a counting problem with restriction of pairing and ordering. Three questions can be abstracted like this.

The first one is that, imagining some people half holding bills of 100 and the others holding 50 dollars (every single people hold exactly one bill) want to buy movie tickets, which are sold in 50 dollars each. But the ticket box has no money left for change. So how to queue these people so that everyone get his/her ticket and change?

The second one is that how many ways can we push in and pop out n instances operating on a stack?

The third one is that in how many ways can we divide a convex n+2-gon into triangles with n-1 diagonal lines?

These questions has two restrictions. First, actions or lines must be paired, like people who hold bills of 50 and those hold 100, push in a particular instance and pop it out. And if you regard diagonal lines as left parenthese and normal edges as No.(from 1 to n+2, of course), they must be paired too. Second, these problems have a premise: ticket box must firstly recieve a 50 dollars bill; an instance must be pushed in before get popped out; in the somehow wierd-looking expression constituted of numbers and parentheses, if we read it from the right end, the left parentheses must be preceded by a number.

If we ignore these restrictions, the solution will be simple and intuitive: we are just picking n places in 2n choices.

While discussing the restriction, I use the polygon example. If one possibility violate the restrictions, there must be one digit or parenthese sit in this position: looking at its right, it should see one more left parentheses than number. So if we change all the numbers on its left to left parentheses and left parentheses to numbers, we will obtain a sequence containing n+1 left parentheses and n-1 numbers. PS: We intentionally ignore the two numbers sitting on the right end of the whole sequence for it is the premise of composing the sequence. This makes the number of numbers decreases from n+2 to n.

With one more step, which is proving in any case, we cannot compose a qualified sequence with n+1 left parentheses and n-1 numbers, we can finally prove that the amount of unqualified possibilities is equal to total amount of sequences we can make out of n+1 parentheses and n-1 numbers.

After the subduce, we get the right answer.

The second one

h(n)= h(0)h(n-1)+h(1)h(n-2) + … + h(n-1)h(0) (n>=2)
This expression is extremely useful to explain the pattern of how binary tree behave. We can imagine a root with zero left-child and a bunch of descendent on the right side; one left-child;…; and finally no child on the right and n-1 descendent on the left.

So we can build h(n) kinds of binary trees with n element, clear and intuitive.
