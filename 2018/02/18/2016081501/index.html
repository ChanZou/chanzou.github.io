How to Fight Imbalanced Data (1)

By Chan Zou

2018-02-18 更新日期:2018-02-18

文章目錄

Background

Nearly all kinds of data sets are intrinsically imbalanced - and this is what makes pattern recognition especially interesitng. I have been working on an extremely skewed data set recently. What yield from single calssifier (whose ‘class_wieght’ was set to be ‘balanced’, certainly) clearly is not satisfying. After some research, I summarize effective methods to fight imbalanced data.

Difficulties

  1. Absolute rarity. The imbalance problem becomes more destructive when the absolute number of minor instances is small. I would rather have a data set that contains 10,000,000:1,000 than 1,000:10. As some studies turns out, data set complexity is the primary factor that hinder learning, which is amplyfied by relative imbalance.
  2. Overlapping.
  3. Lack of representative data.
  4. Small disjuncts. Small disjuncts are closely related to the within-class imbalance. They are rules that cover a small cluster of instances pertaining to the main concept. So noise may influence disjuncts in the minority class. So the validation of the clusters corresponding to minority class becomes important.
  5. Subconcepts. Which ‘dilute’ rare instances furthermore.

Some Advice

  1. Use ensemble methods. There is a reason why people always emphasize on team work. The case is the same in machine learning. I suppose readers of this article are quite familiar with primary voting mechanism. Here is an example of boosting algorithm:

    • Train model M1 with raw training data.
    • Train model M2 on a dataset consisting of half obervations M1 made correct predictions and half not.
    • Train model M3 on observations M1 and M2 disagree on.
      Other boosting methods may apply as well.

  2. Oversampling and Undersampling
    As cliche as it sounds, oversampling and downsampling still hold places in imblance learning. Imbalanced-learning may facilitate developers to explore different approaches. But my experiments told me that if your data is highly skewed (higher than 100:1), oversampling can do little.

  3. Anomaly / Novelty Detection
    Sometimes changing view of points can be more beneficial. Unsupervised learning may divide data into quite convincing groups.

  4. Something I Personally Love
    One particular technique I like is to ‘divide and conquer’. Suppose we have positive:negative = 1:100 then what we can do is to randomly divide negative observations into N (maybe 100) groups and train N models on the concanecation of each group and positive observations. Finally, train an ensemble model (maybe logistic regression) upon all primary models.

Other techniques such as regulation and class weights can relieve negative effect brought by imbalanced data as well. They are oftenly embedded in models.
